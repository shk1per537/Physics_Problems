## 6. Field at a point from a system of charges

We are analyzing the electric field generated by a system of two uneven point charges. According to the principle of superposition, the total electric field ($\vec{E}$) at any point in space is the vector sum of the individual electric fields created by each charge.

The fundamental formula for the electric field vector of a single point charge ($q$) at a position $\vec{r}$ is:

$$
\vec{E} = k \cdot \frac{q}{|\vec{r}|^3} \vec{r}
$$

*Note: The electric field is a vector field, meaning we must break it down into its $x$ and $y$ components (using unit vectors $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$) to properly add the contributions from multiple charges.*

---

### Part A: General Field Vectors

**Given positions:**
* Charge 1 ($q_1$) = $+q$ at $(-a, 0)$
* Charge 2 ($q_2$) = $+2q$ at $(a, 0)$

**1. The general field $\vec{E}(x,y)$:**
For an arbitrary point $(x,y)$, the distance vectors from the charges are $\vec{r}_1 = (x+a)\hat{i} + y\hat{j}$ and $\vec{r}_2 = (x-a)\hat{i} + y\hat{j}$. Applying superposition:

$$
\vec{E}(x,y) = kq \left[ \frac{(x+a)\hat{i} + y\hat{j}}{((x+a)^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \right] + k(2q) \left[ \frac{(x-a)\hat{i} + y\hat{j}}{((x-a)^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \right]
$$

**2. The field along the y-axis $\vec{E}(0,y)$:**
Substitute $x = 0$ into the general formula. The distances simplify to $(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}$ for both charges:

$$
\vec{E}(0,y) = \frac{kq}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \left[ (a\hat{i} + y\hat{j}) + 2(-a\hat{i} + y\hat{j}) \right]
$$

$$
\vec{E}(0,y) = \frac{kq}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} (-a\hat{i} + 3y\hat{j})
$$

**3. The field along the x-axis $\vec{E}(x,0)$:**
Substitute $y = 0$ into the general formula. The y-component vanishes entirely:

$$
\vec{E}(x,0) = kq \left[ \frac{x+a}{|x+a|^3} + \frac{2(x-a)}{|x-a|^3} \right] \hat{i}
$$

### Part B: Conditions for Zero Field

We need to find where $E_y = 0$, $E_x = 0$, and $\vec{E} = 0$.

* **Condition for $E_y = 0$:** The y-component only exists if $y \neq 0$. Therefore, $E_y = 0$ everywhere along the **x-axis ($y = 0$)**.
* **Condition for $E_x = 0$:** This requires the forces from the two positive charges to directly oppose each other, which only happens on the x-axis *between* the charges (where $-a < x < a$). Setting the magnitudes equal:
  
$$
\frac{kq}{(x+a)^2} = \frac{k(2q)}{(a-x)^2} \implies (a-x)^2 = 2(x+a)^2
$$
Solving for $x$ in the valid range gives $x = a \frac{1-\sqrt{2}}{1+\sqrt{2}} = -a(3 - 2\sqrt{2}) \approx -0.172a$.

* **Condition for $\vec{E} = 0$:** The total field is zero only where both components are zero simultaneously. This occurs at a single point: **$(-0.172a, 0)$**.

### Part C: Numerical Calculation

**Given values:**
* $a = 0.2 \text{ m}$
* $y = 0.3 \text{ m}$ (Assuming $x=0$ to find $\vec{E}(0,y)$ based on standard problem formatting)
* $q = 2 \text{ }\mu\text{C} = 2 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C}$
* Coulomb's constant ($k$) $\approx 8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2$

Let's substitute these into our formula for $\vec{E}(0,y)$:
Denominator: $(0.2^2 + 0.3^2)^{3/2} = (0.04 + 0.09)^{3/2} = (0.13)^{3/2} \approx 0.04687 \text{ m}^3$
Constant multiplier: $kq = (8.99 \times 10^9) \cdot (2 \times 10^{-6}) = 17980 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}$

$$
\vec{E}(0,0.3) = \frac{17980}{0.04687} (-0.2\hat{i} + 3(0.3)\hat{j})
$$

$$
\vec{E}(0,0.3) \approx 383614 \cdot (-0.2\hat{i} + 0.9\hat{j})
$$

$$
\vec{E} \approx -7.67 \times 10^4 \hat{i} + 3.45 \times 10^5 \hat{j} \text{ N/C}
$$

### Part D: Limit Investigation ($y \gg a$)

When the observation point is very far away on the y-axis ($y \gg a$), $a^2$ becomes negligible compared to $y^2$. The denominator $(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}$ simplifies to $(y^2)^{3/2} = y^3$.

$$
\vec{E}(0,y) \approx \frac{kq}{y^3} (-a\hat{i} + 3y\hat{j}) = -\frac{kqa}{y^3}\hat{i} + \frac{3kq}{y^2}\hat{j}
$$

* The y-component is proportional to $1/y^2$ and represents the field of a point charge of magnitude $+3q$ (the total net charge of the system).
* The x-component is proportional to $1/y^3$, representing the dipole contribution due to the spatial separation of the charges. 
* At extreme distances, the $1/y^3$ term vanishes much faster, and the system behaves almost exactly like a single point charge of $+3q$.

### Final Answer

* **General Vectors:** $\vec{E}(0,y) = \frac{kq}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} (-a\hat{i} + 3y\hat{j})$.
* **Zero Field:** The total electric field is exactly zero at coordinate **$(-a(3-2\sqrt{2}), 0)$**.
* **Calculated Field:** At $(0, 0.3\text{m})$, the field is approximately **$(-7.67 \times 10^4 \hat{i} + 3.45 \times 10^5 \hat{j}) \text{ N/C}$**.
* **Limit behavior:** As $y \gg a$, the system acts increasingly like a single point charge of **$+3q$**.
