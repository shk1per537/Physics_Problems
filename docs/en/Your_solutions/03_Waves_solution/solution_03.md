## 3. Electrostatic Equilibrium

We are finding the equilibrium position for a third charge placed between two other charges. For a charge to be in electrostatic equilibrium, the net electric force acting on it must be zero. According to Coulomb's Law, the magnitude of the force ($F$) between two point charges is:

$$
F = k \cdot \frac{|q_a \cdot q_b|}{r^2}
$$

To achieve equilibrium for the third charge ($q_3$), the repulsive force from the first charge ($F_{13}$) must be exactly equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the repulsive force from the second charge ($F_{23}$).

*Note: Because all three charges are positive, $q_1$ and $q_2$ will both repel $q_3$. The only way these two forces can oppose each other and cancel out is if $q_3$ is placed somewhere on the straight line directly between them.*

---

### Part A: Setting up the Equation

**Given:**
* Charge 1 ($q_1$) = +4.0 C
* Charge 2 ($q_2$) = +9.0 C
* Charge 3 ($q_3$) = +1.0 C
* Total distance between $q_1$ and $q_2$ ($d$) = 2.0 m

Let $x$ be the distance from $q_1$ to the equilibrium position of $q_3$. Consequently, the distance from $q_2$ to $q_3$ will be $(d - x)$.

Equating the magnitudes of the two opposing forces acting on $q_3$:

$$
F_{13} = F_{23}
$$

$$
k \cdot \frac{q_1 \cdot q_3}{x^2} = k \cdot \frac{q_2 \cdot q_3}{(d - x)^2}
$$

We can simplify this equation dramatically by canceling out Coulomb's constant ($k$) and the test charge ($q_3$) from both sides:

$$
\frac{q_1}{x^2} = \frac{q_2}{(d - x)^2}
$$

### Part B: Solving for Distance

Now, let's substitute our known values into the simplified equation:

$$
\frac{4.0}{x^2} = \frac{9.0}{(2.0 - x)^2}
$$

To solve for $x$, we can take the square root of both sides. Since we know the position must physically lie between the two charges, we only need to consider the positive roots:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{4.0}}{\sqrt{x^2}} = \frac{\sqrt{9.0}}{\sqrt{(2.0 - x)^2}}
$$

$$
\frac{2}{x} = \frac{3}{2.0 - x}
$$

Next, we cross-multiply to solve for $x$:

$$
2 \cdot (2.0 - x) = 3 \cdot x
$$

$$
4.0 - 2x = 3x
$$

$$
4.0 = 5x
$$

$$
x = \frac{4.0}{5} = 0.8 \text{ m}
$$

### Final Answer

* The equilibrium position for charge $q_3$ is exactly **0.8 meters** away from the +4C charge ($q_1$).
* Equivalently, this places it $2.0 - 0.8 =$ **1.2 meters** away from the +9C charge ($q_2$).
