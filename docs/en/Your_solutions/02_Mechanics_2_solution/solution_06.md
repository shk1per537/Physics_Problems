## 6. Energy Dissipation

We are looking at a tennis ball that loses a fraction of its mechanical energy with every bounce. We need to find its maximum height after the second bounce.

**Given:**
* Initial height ($h_0$) = 2.0 m
* Energy lost per bounce = 30%
* Energy retained per bounce = 100% - 30% = 70% (or 0.70)

---

### Step 1: Relate mechanical energy to height

At the very top of its bounce, the tennis ball momentarily stops moving. This means its kinetic energy is zero, and all of its mechanical energy ($E$) is stored as gravitational potential energy. 

The formula for gravitational potential energy is: $E = mgh$

Since the mass of the ball ($m$) and the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) remain constant, the maximum height ($h$) is directly proportional to the total mechanical energy ($E$). If the ball loses 30% of its energy, it will also lose 30% of its maximum bounce height.

### Step 2: Calculate the height after the first bounce ($h_1$)

After the first bounce, the ball retains 70% of its initial energy ($E_0$). Therefore, it will rise to 70% of its initial height:

$$
h_1 = 0.70 \cdot h_0
$$

Substitute the initial height:

$$
h_1 = 0.70 \cdot 2.0 = 1.4 \text{ m}
$$

### Step 3: Calculate the height after the second bounce ($h_2$)

After the second bounce, the ball again loses 30% of the energy it had *during the first bounce*. It retains 70% of $E_1$. So, it will rise to 70% of $h_1$:

$$
h_2 = 0.70 \cdot h_1
$$

Substitute the value of $h_1$ we just found:

$$
h_2 = 0.70 \cdot 1.4 = 0.98 \text{ m}
$$

> **Note:** You can also calculate this in a single step using an exponential decay formula: $h_n = h_0 \cdot (0.70)^n$. For the second bounce ($n=2$): $h_2 = 2.0 \cdot (0.70)^2 = 2.0 \cdot 0.49 = 0.98$ m.

### Final Answer

After the second bounce, the tennis ball rises to a height of **0.98 meters** (or 98 cm).
