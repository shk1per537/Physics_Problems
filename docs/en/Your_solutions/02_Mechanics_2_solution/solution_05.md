## 5. Inelastic Collision

This problem involves a perfectly inelastic collision (where two objects stick together). We need to find the final speed of the combined mass and determine if kinetic energy is conserved.

**Given:**
* Mass of the runner ($m_1$) = 70 kg
* Initial velocity of the runner ($v_1$) = 3 m/s
* Mass of the cart ($m_2$) = 140 kg
* Initial velocity of the cart ($v_2$) = 0 m/s (stationary)

---

### Step 1: Find the final speed using Conservation of Momentum

In any collision where there are no external forces, the total momentum of the system is conserved. 

$$
\text{Initial Momentum} = \text{Final Momentum}
$$

$$
m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f
$$

Since the cart is initially stationary, its momentum is zero ($140 \cdot 0 = 0$). We can plug in the rest of our values:

$$
(70)(3) + 0 = (70 + 140)v_f
$$

$$
210 = 210v_f
$$

$$
v_f = \frac{210}{210} = 1
$$

> **Note:** The final speed of the cart with the runner is exactly 1 m/s.

### Step 2: Check the Conservation of Kinetic Energy

To see if kinetic energy is conserved, we calculate the total kinetic energy before and after the collision. The formula for kinetic energy is $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

**Initial Kinetic Energy ($KE_i$):**
Only the runner is moving before the collision.

$$
KE_i = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2
$$

$$
KE_i = \frac{1}{2}(70)(3)^2 = 35 \cdot 9 = 315 \text{ J}
$$

**Final Kinetic Energy ($KE_f$):**
Both the runner and the cart are moving together at $v_f = 1$ m/s.

$$
KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)v_f^2
$$

$$
KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(210)(1)^2 = 105 \cdot 1 = 105 \text{ J}
$$

### Final Answer

* **Final Speed:** The final speed of the cart with the runner is **1 m/s**.
* **Is Kinetic Energy Conserved?** **No.** The initial kinetic energy was 315 Joules, and the final kinetic energy is only 105 Joules. In a perfectly inelastic collision, kinetic energy is never conserved. The "lost" energy (210 Joules) is transformed into other forms of energy, such as sound, thermal energy, and the mechanical work done by the runner's muscles and joints absorbing the impact.
