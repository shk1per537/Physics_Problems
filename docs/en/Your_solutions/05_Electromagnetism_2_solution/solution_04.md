## 4. Magnetic Torque

We are calculating the magnitude of the magnetic torque acting on a current-carrying loop placed in a uniform magnetic field. The fundamental formula for the magnitude of magnetic torque ($\tau$) is:

$$
\tau = N \cdot I \cdot A \cdot B \cdot \sin(\theta)
$$

Where:
* $N$ is the number of turns in the loop (assumed to be 1 since it's a single loop)
* $I$ is the current flowing through the loop
* $A$ is the area of the loop
* $B$ is the magnetic field strength
* $\theta$ is the angle between the area vector (the normal or perpendicular line to the loop's surface) and the magnetic field vector.

*Note: Pay close attention to the angle. The problem states the field is applied **parallel to the plane** of the loop. Because the normal vector is perpendicular to the loop's plane, the angle between this normal vector and the magnetic field is exactly $90^\circ$.*

---

### Part A: Calculating the Area

**Given Constants:**
* Dimensions = $10 \text{ cm}$ by $5 \text{ cm}$
* Current ($I$) = $2 \text{ A}$
* Magnetic field ($B$) = $0.3 \text{ T}$
* Angle ($\theta$) = $90^\circ$

First, let's convert the dimensions to standard SI units (meters) and calculate the area ($A$) of the rectangular loop:
* Length ($l$) = $0.1 \text{ m}$
* Width ($w$) = $0.05 \text{ m}$

$$
A = l \cdot w = 0.1 \cdot 0.05
$$

$$
A = 0.005 \text{ m}^2
$$

### Part B: Calculating the Torque

Now we can substitute all our known values into the torque equation:

$$
\tau = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 0.005 \cdot 0.3 \cdot \sin(90^\circ)
$$

Since $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$:

$$
\tau = 2 \cdot 0.005 \cdot 0.3 \cdot 1
$$

First, multiply the current by the area:

$$
\tau = 0.01 \cdot 0.3
$$

$$
\tau = 0.003 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}
$$

### Final Answer

* The magnitude of the magnetic torque on the rectangular loop is **$0.003 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$** (or $3 \times 10^{-3} \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$).
* Because the magnetic field is parallel to the plane of the loop, the sine of the angle is at its maximum (1). Therefore, this configuration experiences the **maximum possible magnetic torque**.
