## 9. Refraction (Snell's Law)

We are calculating the angle at which a light ray bends (refracts) when passing from one transparent medium into another. The fundamental formula governing this behavior is Snell's Law:

$$
n_1 \cdot \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \cdot \sin(\theta_2)
$$

Where:
* $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the indices of refraction for the first and second mediums, respectively.
* $\theta_1$ is the angle of incidence (measured from the normal line perpendicular to the surface).
* $\theta_2$ is the angle of refraction (also measured from the normal line).

To find the angle of refraction ($\theta_2$), we need to rearrange the equation to isolate $\sin(\theta_2)$ and then use the inverse sine function.

*Note: Because the light is moving from an optically less dense medium (air, $n=1.00$) to a more dense medium (glass, $n=1.50$), the ray will bend toward the normal. This means our final calculated angle must be smaller than the initial $30^\circ$ angle.*

---

### Part A: Calculating the Angle

**Given Constants:**
* Index of refraction of air ($n_1$) = 1.00
* Index of refraction of glass ($n_2$) = 1.50
* Angle of incidence ($\theta_1$) = $30^\circ$

Let's substitute our known values into Snell's Law:

$$
1.00 \cdot \sin(30^\circ) = 1.50 \cdot \sin(\theta_2)
$$

We know that the sine of $30^\circ$ is exactly $0.5$. Substituting this in:

$$
1.00 \cdot 0.5 = 1.50 \cdot \sin(\theta_2)
$$

$$
0.5 = 1.50 \cdot \sin(\theta_2)
$$

Now, we isolate $\sin(\theta_2)$ by dividing both sides by 1.50:

$$
\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{0.5}{1.50}
$$

$$
\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.3333
$$

To find the actual angle $\theta_2$, we take the inverse sine ($\arcsin$ or $\sin^{-1}$) of this value:

$$
\theta_2 = \arcsin\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)
$$

$$
\theta_2 \approx 19.47^\circ
$$

### Final Answer

* The angle of refraction as the light ray enters the glass is approximately **19.47°**.
* As expected, the light ray bends toward the normal line (19.47° < 30°) due to entering a medium with a higher index of refraction.
