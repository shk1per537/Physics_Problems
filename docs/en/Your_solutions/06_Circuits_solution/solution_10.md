## 10. Average Current

We are calculating the average electric current produced during a lightning strike. Average current ($I_{avg}$) is defined as the total amount of electric charge ($Q$) that flows through a specific point divided by the total time ($\Delta t$) it takes for that charge to flow. 

The fundamental formula is:

$$
I_{avg} = \frac{Q}{\Delta t}
$$

*Note: A millisecond (ms) is one-thousandth of a second ($10^{-3}\ \text{s}$). It is crucial to convert this time into standard SI units (seconds) before performing the calculation to ensure the resulting current is in standard Amperes (A).*

---

### Part A: Calculating the Current

**Given Constants:**
* Total charge ($Q$) = $30\ \text{C}$
* Time ($\Delta t$) = $2\ \text{ms} = 2 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s}$

Let's substitute our known values into the average current formula:

$$
I_{avg} = \frac{30}{2 \times 10^{-3}}
$$

To solve this, we can divide the standard numbers first:

$$
I_{avg} = 15 \cdot \frac{1}{10^{-3}}
$$

Bringing the power of 10 to the numerator changes the sign of the exponent:

$$
I_{avg} = 15 \times 10^3\ \text{A}
$$

$$
I_{avg} = 15,000\ \text{A}
$$

### Final Answer

* The average current of the lightning bolt is **$15,000\ \text{A}$** (which can also be written as **$15\ \text{kA}$**). 
* This massive amount of current illustrates why lightning strikes are so incredibly powerful and destructive, even though they last for only a tiny fraction of a second.
