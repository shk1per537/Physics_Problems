## 3. Proportionality

We are given the Universal Law of Gravitation:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

We need to find the new force, let's call it $F_{new}$, when the distance $r$ is doubled and both masses ($m_1$ and $m_2$) are halved.

---

### Step 1: Define the new variables

Let the new distance be $r'$ and the new masses be $m_1'$ and $m_2'$:

$$
r' = 2r
$$

$$
m_1' = \frac{1}{2}m_1, \qquad m_2' = \frac{1}{2}m_2
$$

### Step 2: Substitute into the formula

The formula for the new gravitational force is:

$$
F_{new} = G \frac{m_1' m_2'}{(r')^2}
$$

Substitute the new variables into this equation:

$$
F_{new} = G \frac{\left(\frac{1}{2}m_1\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}m_2\right)}{(2r)^2}
$$

### Step 3: Simplify the expression

Simplify the numerator (multiply the masses):

$$
\frac{1}{2}m_1 \cdot \frac{1}{2}m_2 = \frac{1}{4}m_1 m_2
$$

Simplify the denominator (square the new distance):

$$
(2r)^2 = 4r^2
$$

Now put the simplified parts back together:

$$
F_{new} = G \frac{\frac{1}{4}m_1 m_2}{4r^2}
$$

### Step 4: Find the factor of change

Divide the fractions to isolate the original formula components:

$$
F_{new} = \frac{\frac{1}{4}}{4} \cdot G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

$$
F_{new} = \frac{1}{16} \cdot G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

Since the original force is $F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$, we can substitute $F$ back in:

$$
F_{new} = \frac{1}{16} F
$$

### Final Answer

The gravitational force changes by a factor of **1/16** (it becomes 16 times smaller).
